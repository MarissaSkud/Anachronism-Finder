FAQs

Wordsworth is a tool to help historical fiction authors avoid linguistic anachronisms. You can compare a passage from your story to a corpus of fiction from the decade you're writing about, or look up whether a specific bigram is found in fiction from that decade.

About Wordsworth in General

Where did you come up with the idea for this app?

Wordsworth is inspired by the work of Ben Schmidt, a digital-humanities professor who, in the early 2010s, wrote about trying to identify linguistic anachronisms in historical fiction screenplays by comparing them to texts written in the period they were set. Schmidt's program and corpus is not publicly available, so we decided to try to create an approximation of it.


Your app is meant for fiction writers, not for poets, and your corpus does not include poetry. So why is it called Wordsworth?

We can't resist a literary pun and we told ourselves that no one else would overthink this.


I have a question that isn't answered on this page / I think I've discovered a bug on Wordsworth. How can I get in touch with you?

Email the app's creator at marissa (dot) skudlarek (at) gmail.com, or submit an Issue on Wordsworth's GitHub page.


About Wordsworth's Features


What is a bigram?

A bigram (pronounced "BY-gram") is a linguistic term for a phrase of two words.


What are the Google Ngram charts and where do they come from?

The Google Books Ngram Viewer is a Google feature that uses the Google Books corpus to graph word and phrase frequencies over time. These graphs can be embedded in other websites using iframes. Wordsworth's Google Ngram charts default to pulling from Google's "English Fiction" corpus.


How does the Discount Words feature work?

The Discount Words feature allows you to tell the system to ignore certain words in your passage (words you may already know are anachronistic) before analyzing it. For instance, Mary Robinette Kowal's fantasy novel Shades of Milk and Honey is heavily influenced by the works of Jane Austen, but it features a hero named "Mr. Vincent" and a magic system known as "glamour". Neither the words "vincent" or "glamour" appear in our 1810s corpus, so it would make sense for Kowal to tell the system to ignore those words if she ran her text through the app.


About Wordsworth's Corpus

What novels are in the comparison corpus?

You can view a list of all novels in the corpus here.


Where have you sourced the texts for the comparison corpus?

All texts come from Project Gutenberg.


What qualifies a novel for your comparison corpus?

Corpus novels should have been written in English between the years of 1800 and 1923, and have had a realistic, contemporary setting at the time they were published. (But we define "realistic" and "contemporary" in a loose way.) For instance, the novels of Austen, Gaskell, Forster, Wharton, Alcott, Trollope, etc.


Madame Bovary / Anna Karenina / Effi Briest is a classic! Why isn't it in your corpus?

None of these novels qualify because they were originally written in French, Russian, and German, respectively. Also, you should take a hard look at why you are so into novels where women commit adultery and then die in misery.


If your corpus is focused on realistic fiction, why does it include Dracula and a Sherlock Holmes novel?

We have decided to include some genre fiction that takes place in a "realistic" setting (as opposed to a fantasy or historical setting). Dracula may be a fantasy/horror novel and the Sherlock Holmes stories may be mystery fiction, but they are recognizably set in late Victorian England and offer a wealth of incidental information about how language was used in that era. They have also proved extremely influential to modern fiction writers. We want Wordsworth to be useful to writers of steampunk stories or mystery stories or gothic stories -- not just writers of "literary" fiction.


Are you aware that the version of The Tenant of Wildfell Hall in your corpus is an abridged version?

Yes, to our chagrin -- because that's the version that was available on Project Gutenberg. (Justice for Anne BrontÃ«!) If you know of a publicly available plaintext version of the unabridged version, please message us.


The bestselling American novel of the 1800s was Ben-Hur. Why isn't it in your corpus?

Ben-Hur is a work of historical fiction that takes place in a Biblical setting. Therefore, we worried that its language would skew our corpus results in a way that wouldn't be helpful to someone who's using our app to help them write a story that is set in the 1880s. This is also why we have not included the historical novels of Walter Scott.


I am writing a World War II novel. Why doesn't your corpus include works from the 1940s?

As of 2019, the public domain in the United States ends in 1923. For legal reasons, we have therefore decided not to analyze and store word-frequency data from novels written in 1924 and later. As new 1920s novels enter the public domain in the years to come, we will try to add them to our corpus. If you feel strongly that the U.S. public domain laws are ridiculous, lobby your Congressional representatives.


Ulysses is the greatest novel of the 20th century and it is public domain and it is set on a specific day in 1900s Dublin! What do you have against James Joyce?

Joyce's use of language is just too innovative for our humble little app and might unduly skew the corpus results.


I've thought of another text that isn't in your corpus, but it should be. How can I add it?

Email us at marissa skudlarek at gmail dot com and suggest it! If it otherwise fits the corpus parameters, we'll look into adding it in a subsequent release. However, we do not plan to develop a feature to allow users to add texts to the corpus themselves.
